### **Origins: Where It All Began**
Nymbl's story begins with Martyn starting as an intern at BackOffice Associates in **2006\.** He found himself immersed in the intricate world of data migration and data governance. BackOffice excelled in transferring data from outdated legacy systems into modern platforms like SAP, requiring deep expertise in back-end systems and business processes.

It was here that Martyn began experimenting with early app development, tackling complex challenges with what we now recognize as low-code/no-code solutions. The team’s ingenuity sparked innovations like “Demo Jams,” where they competed to showcase groundbreaking apps.

These projects not only solved business problems but ignited Martyn's vision for something far greater...

### **Nymbl 1.0: The First Leap**
In **2012**, BackOffice was acquired, signaling a time for change. Martyn, along with his friend (and biggest rival) Cody, took the leap to create the first version of Nymbl. Initially, the company aimed to deliver services in the emerging low-code space, operating as a service arm for Zudy, a company focused on app development products.

Nymbl grew rapidly, taking on critical client accounts and demonstrating expertise in the new technology landscape. The team's success was so significant that it led to a merger with Zudy.

This union helped Zudy expand from 10 to 175 customers in less than 4 years, and grow its team from 10 to over 125 employees. For Martyn, this was a period of intense growth—he learned the ropes of sales, product development, and scaling a company. But the desire to create something uniquely his own never faded. Joy also had joined the Zudy business again this time in sales operations role, learning the backbone of running software business and all the moving parts needed to support that.

### **The Pivot: Nymbl 2.0**
After four years, Martyn, with Joy, made a bold decision: to relaunch Nymbl as an independent, tech-agnostic company. This new version of Nymbl aimed to focus on client needs rather than selling a single platform, providing tailored solutions across various low-code platforms like Retool, Bubble, Vinyl & Mendix.

The strategy paid off quickly. Within just six months, Nymbl secured key clients and validated its vision to be more than just a development shop. The company was positioned to tackle enterprise challenges with agility and innovation, reshaping how app development services were delivered.

* * *

The next transformative step came with Blair joining the team. With Blair’s expertise, Nymbl evolved from being a development-focused company to a strategic advisory partner.

The company began addressing high-level business challenges, delivering not just applications but also insights that shaped enterprise strategies.

This shift reinforced Nymbl’s core identity: a partner capable of delivering exceptional results across platforms while helping clients navigate complex digital transformations.

# First Week (In Work)

### Welcome to Your First Week at Nymbl
Welcome, Nymbling! We’re excited to have you here! Your first week is all about getting you set up, connected, and ready to dive in. Here’s the plan:

**Day 1: HR & IT Setup**
*   **HR Onboarding**:
    *   Complete your Gusto profile for payroll and benefits.
    *   Review PTO policy and working hours with your manager.
    *   Meet with HR to get onboarded and run through checklist in Clickup.
    *   Complete tasks in onboarding checklist in Click Up.
*   **IT Setup**:
    *   Get access to tools: ClickUp, NOS, Slack, Zoom, Google Drive, NOS and more.
    *   Set up accounts and familiarize yourself with key platforms.

**Day 2: Your Role & Basics**
*   Meet with your manager to review goals, responsibilities, and tools.
*   Complete new hire tasks:
    *   Update your email signature.
    *   Submit compliance forms (e.g., I-9 for US employees).
    *   Watch overview and training videos to understand Nymbl’s mission, processes, and best practices.
*   Introduce yourself on Slack and start connecting.

**Day 3: Team & Tools**
*   Meet and greets with our team and start building relationships.
*   Update LinkedIn to share your new role.
*   Complete required HR documents and submit your bio and headshot for our directory.

**Day 4-5: Training & Reflection**
*   Complete systems training (e.g., NOS).
*   Dive into self-study materials: app development, web development, and the Nymbl Overview Deck.
*   Wrap up the week with an onboarding survey and share feedback to help us improve.

# The Nymbl Y

![](https://t36061546.p.clickup-attachments.com/t36061546/989bf885-5c56-4a5d-94d7-ac45d1ab453a/Screenshot%202025-01-04%20at%202.18.12%E2%80%AFPM.png)

At Nymbl, careers split into two clear paths—Consulting and Technology. Both are essential, equally valued, and designed to let you thrive based on your strengths and passions.

**Consulting Path**
The consulting path is for those who lead with strategy and relationships. You work closely with clients, uncovering their challenges, solving problems, and optimizing processes. Your focus: delivering insights that drive real-world impact.

**Technology Path**
The technology path is for builders and innovators. You dive deep into coding, architecture, and design, creating the systems and tools that power Nymbl’s solutions. Your focus: developing technologies that solve complex problems and scale with ease.

The "Y" model ensures you choose a path that fits your skills and interests while keeping Nymbl strong on both fronts. Whether you’re advising or building, you’re driving impact and growing your career.

# How we work

### How We Work at Nymbl
We keep it simple: Learn, Build, Grow. This is how we turn ideas into impact, creating solutions that scale with our clients’ ambitions.

**1\. Learn**
Understand first, act second. This phase is about clarity:
*   **Discovery**: What does the client want? What’s in their way?
*   **Research**: Dive deep into the market, the industry, and the problem.
*   **Mapping**: Visualize workflows, processes, and the future state.
*   **Proof of Concept**: Test ideas fast—validate, refine, and align.
*   **Strategic Goals**: Define KPIs so success is measurable, not abstract.
By the end, the client’s roadmap is clear. Everyone knows where we’re headed.

**2\. Build**
This is execution, done right:
*   **Agile Sprints**: Small steps, quick wins. Adapt as we go.
*   **Collaboration**: Work side-by-side with the client. Transparency breeds trust.
*   **Feedback Loops**: Regular demos keep everyone aligned.
*   **Quality First**: Test early, test often. Fix issues before they’re problems.
*   **Documentation**: Capture the process for smooth handoffs and scalability.
We stay flexible but focused, ensuring the solution evolves with the project.

**3\. Grow**
Delivery isn’t the end—it’s the beginning:
*   **Support**: Stay hands-on to keep things running smoothly.
*   **Performance**: Measure. Monitor. Optimize.
*   **Iterate**: Make it better with every user insight.
*   **Scale**: Build for what’s next—bigger teams, more users, new features.
*   **Partnership**: Stick around. Be the partner who helps the client thrive.

**Why It Works**
Learn, Build, Grow isn’t just a process—it’s how we create lasting impact. Grounded in strategy, built with flexibility, and designed to scale. Solutions that work today and evolve for tomorrow.

For more information, refer to the [Nymbl How We Work page](https://www.nymbl.app/how-we-work).

# What is the 15 Min Rule?

### **What**
The **15-Minute Rule** is a problem-solving approach where a person spends 15 focused minutes attempting to solve an issue independently. During this time, they document the steps taken, search for solutions and attempt to resolve the issue using every available resource.

If, after 15 minutes, the the person is still stuck, they seek assistance from a colleague, mentor, or relevant support channels in Slack. (Team, Department, Platform, etc..)​
### **Why**
This rule is designed to prevent excessive time loss on difficult problems by encouraging developers to try solving issues independently before seeking help. It builds problem-solving skills and autonomy while ensuring that developers do not waste too much time on intractable issues. It also fosters collaboration when necessary, as asking for help after making a genuine effort increases the chances of learning from peers and receiving targeted support​(
### **Who**
The 15-Minute Rule is intended for ANYONE at Nymbl who want to improve their problem-solving skills. It’s particularly useful in team settings where collaboration is key, allowing people to balance self-sufficiency with knowing when to seek guidance.

# What is Guidance?

**What** planned number of hours an hourly consultant is expected to work on a specific project or task within a given timeframe.

**Why** Resource Management: Helps in effective allocation and utilization of resources.
Budget Control: Ensures that project costs stay within the planned budget.
Workload Balance: Prevents overworking or underutilizing consultants

![](https://t36061546.p.clickup-attachments.com/t36061546/9ffe03ca-7152-4baa-9176-5acc0b1f8d24/Screenshot%202024-08-29%20at%202.42.36%E2%80%AFPM.png)

In this example, a full time employee is available to work for **160** hours. (4 Weeks x 40 Hours a Week = 160)

Of the **160** hours, they have **136** hours of planned guidance across multiple projects for the month.

Around the half way point of the month, they have logged **80** Hours of Actual hours in the Time & Entry System. This is 50% of their total availability for the month.

**Availability** How many hours someone is available to work in a given month
**Guidance** How many hours have been planned / assigned to a person in a given month
**Actuals** How many actual hours have been logged in the Time & Entry System

# How to Submit PTO

All Nymbl employees are expected to follow the procedure documented below when requesting time off.  
# **Under 5 Consecutive Days** 
 1. **Email a request for approval** from manager and cc: [hr@nymbl.app](mailto:hr@nymbl.app) with planned PTO dates. 

 2. Request the PTO in the **Time Entry App in MyNOS.** 

 3. Create a **Personal Event** on your calendar:  
*    Indicate your reason for being out in the title:  PTO/International Holiday/Sick/etc. 
*   Set it to **“FREE”** instead of “busy” so it does not block off anyone’s time slots.  
*   Set it to **“All Day”** so it sits at the top of the calendar date.  
*   **Invite** the following Nymbl team members:  
*   Manager,  
*   HR ([HR@nymbl.app](mailto:HR@nymbl.app))  
*   Project Lead(s),  
*   Project Team member(s), 
*   Resourcing Team (Melissa, Chase),  

4\. For RevOps roles: **Ensure guidance for all projects** is up to date and project teams are aligned. 

 5. Ensure all recent time entries in NOS are **entered and signed off** for the week, if necessary. If not, the time will be marked “late”.  

 6. During your PTO, make sure to **update your Slack Status** to OOO with the date range. 

 7. Set up an out of office auto reply in Outlook for the duration of your time off.  

# **5 Consecutive Days or More** 
**_Four (4) weeks' notice is required._** 

1\. **Email a request for approval** from manager and cc: [hr@nymbl.app](mailto:hr@nymbl.app) with planned PTO dates minumum 4 weeks in advance. 

2\. Once you have been approved, request the PTO in the **Time Entry App in MyNOS (**[**Click here**](https://scribehow.com/shared/Submitting_Time_Off_Requests_in_MyNOS__ZjzTU1tWRXmgH3m_OXCbNA) **for walkthrough).** 

3\. Create a **personal Event** on your calendar:  
*    Indicate your reason for being out in the title:  PTO/International Holiday/Sick/etc. 
*   Set it to **“FREE”** instead of “busy” so it does not block off anyone’s time slots.  
*   Set it to **“All Day”** so it sits at the top of the calendar date.  
*   **Invite** **the following** Nymbl team members:  
*   Manager,  
*   HR ([HR@nymbl.app](mailto:HR@nymbl.app))  
*   Project Lead(s),  
*   Project Team member(s),   
*   Resourcing Team (Melissa, Chase), 

4\. Create a **Coverage plan** for each project and communicate it with your teams.  

5\. For RevOps roles: **Ensure guidance for all projects** is up to date. 

6\. Ensure all your **recent time entries** in NOS are entered and **"signed off'** for the week, if necessary.  

7\. During your PTO, make sure to **update your Slack Status** to OOO with the date range. 

8\. Set up an **out of office auto reply in Outlook** for the duration of your time off.

# Context Switching

## 1) Group Similar Tasks (Batching)
**What:** Work on similar tasks in dedicated time blocks (e.g., all emails and Slack, all code reviews).
**Why:** Minimizes “gear change” cost and preserves momentum.
**How (Step by step)**
1. List your common task types (email, writing, reviews, meetings, admin).
2. Assign time blocks on your calendar for each type of task.
3. Queue tasks into the matching block.
4. Use a timer; don’t mix task types inside of a block.
5. Adjust block sizes weekly, based on demand.
* * *
## 2) Set Clear Entry & Exit Points
**What:** Leave breadcrumbs before pausing a task.
**Why:** Faster re-entry, less ramp-up, fewer forgotten details.
**How (Step by step):**
1. Before stopping, write the **very next action** you’ll take.
    1. Use the Comments section if task is a ClickUp ticket, else can use a parking lot list.
2. Jot current state (links, doc section, open tabs).
3. Save a short “DONE / NEXT” note in the task.
4. On return, read the note first—then execute immediately.
5. Update the note again before the next pause.
* * *
## 3) Use a Parking Lot List
**What:** One capture list for stray ideas and requests.
**Why:** Prevents derailment without losing good ideas.
**How (Step by step):**
1. Pick one tool (Notes, Notion, ClickUp's Personal List, other).
2. Create a quick-capture hotkey on all devices.
3. Dump ideas instantly—no categorizing mid-flow.
4. Review the list at scheduled set times (e.g., 11:30 & 4:30).
5. Triage: do (<2 min), schedule, or delegate.
* * *
## 4) Micro-Planning (Daily / Half-Day)
**What:** A 5–10 minute plan for the next block of hours.
**Why:** Reduces reactive jumping; clarifies trade-offs.
**How (Step by step):**
1. Identify your Top 3 outcomes for the period.
2. Estimate time and place them in calendar blocks.
3. Sequence by energy/complexity.
4. Note dependencies and pre-open resources.
5. Midway check: adjust based on reality.
* * *
## 5) Timebox Everything
**What:** Give tasks fixed time limits (e.g., 45 minutes).
**Why:** Adds urgency, prevents scope creep, makes switches intentional.
**How (Step by step):**
1. Pick a realistic duration per task.
2. Set a timer; commit to single-tasking.
3. Stop at the bell; log what’s left.
4. Decide: extend, schedule another box, or drop.
5. Record actual vs. planned to improve estimates.
* * *
## 6) Reduce “Open Loops”
**What:** Externalize all tasks into a trusted system.
**Why:** Frees working memory; smoother context switches.
**How (Step by step):**
1. Do a quick brain dump of everything pending.
2. Convert each item into a clear **next action**.
3. Add due dates/context tags only where helpful.
4. Keep a daily “Today” view small and realistic.
5. Weekly review: close, defer, or delegate.
* * *
## 7) Set Up Clear Workspaces
**What:** Separate environments for different modes of work.
**Why:** Contextual cues reduce friction and temptation.
**How (Step by step):**
1. Create browser profiles (Focus, Comms, Admin).
2. Use separate desktops/spaces per mode.
3. Save window/session presets for each mode.
4. Keep physical desk cues minimal and mode-specific.
5. Automate setup with scripts/shortcuts.
* * *
## 8) Communicate Boundaries
**What:** Make your availability and response expectations explicit.
**Why:** Fewer interruptions and fewer forced switches.
**How (Step by step):**
1. Block focus time on your calendar.
2. Set Slack/Teams status.
    1. Display return time if away for block of time.
    2. Pause notifications for deep focus blocks.
3. Share response SLAs (e.g., “I reply 2–3x/day”).
4. Offer an escalation path for urgent items.
5. Reiterate norms in team standups/handbooks.
* * *
## 9) Do Small Reset Rituals
**What:** A 1–2 minute ritual between contexts.
**Why:** Clears residual attention and boosts next-task engagement.
**How (Step by step):**
1. Stand up, breathe, quick stretch, or short walk.
2. Close previous task tabs/windows.
3. State the next task’s objective out loud or in notes.
4. Open only the resources needed for the next task.
5. Start with a 2–5 minute “easy win” to build traction. Examples:
    1. **Clear email** – archive or reply to 3 quick messages.
    2. **Tidy workspace** – delete or move a few files/folders.
    3. **Capture an idea** – log a stray thought in notes.
    4. **Prep workspace** – open next file/section needed.
* * *
## 10) Track & Learn From Your Switches
**What:** Log when/why you switch for one week.
**Why:** Reveals triggers and fixes with the highest ROI.
**How (Step by step):**
1. Create a simple log (time, from → to, trigger, cost).
2. Capture every unplanned switch for 5–7 days.
3. Tag causes (ping, meeting, boredom, unclear task).
4. Review: find top 2–3 triggers by frequency/impact.
5. Implement one counter measure per week; repeat.

# Family & Medical Leave Act Policy

**📝 FMLA Leave**: Provides eligible employees with up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave in a 12-month period.

**✅ Eligible Reasons**:
👶 Birth, adoption, or foster placement of a child.
🏥 Serious health condition of the employee or a family member (spouse, child, or parent).
🎖️ Certain reasons related to the foreign deployment of a military service member.
🕐 Up to 26 weeks for caring for a covered service member with a serious injury or illness.

**⏳ FMLA Leave Usage**:
*       *   Can be taken in one block or intermittently (separate blocks of time or a reduced schedule).
    *   FMLA is unpaid, but employer-provided paid leave may be used if available.

**📋 Eligibility Criteria**:
*       *   Worked for a covered employer for at least 12 months.
    *   Have at least 1,250 hours of service in the past 12 months.
    *   Employer must have at least 50 employees within 75 miles.

**🏢 Employer Obligations**:
🩺 Maintain group health plan coverage during leave.
💼 Provide the same or a similar job upon return.
🚫 Cannot retaliate or discriminate for taking FMLA leave.

**📅 Requesting Leave**:
*       *   Follow normal [leave request procedures](https://app.clickup.com/36061546/v/dc/12cgba-70733/12cgba-76913).
    *   Provide at least 30 days’ notice when possible.

# Mission (In Work)

**What**
Defines the core purpose of an organization—what it aims to achieve, whom it serves, and how it does so. It often outlines the company’s fundamental goals and values, providing direction and focus for decision-making and strategy.

**Why**
**Guidance:** Provides a clear direction for employees and stakeholders.
**Alignment:** Ensures that all efforts are focused on achieving common goals.
**Motivation:** Inspires and motivates employees by connecting their work to a larger purpose.
**Branding:** Communicates the company’s purpose to customers, partners, and the public.

**Client Mission**

We empower leaders to execute their vision by eliminating the constraints of technology and enabling them to focus on what’s important.

**Employee Mission**

Create a work environment where employees are continuously growing, have meaningful project experiences, and feel connected to a greater mission

A bold vision for the future—a single guiding star that defines where we’re headed. It’s not just a statement; it’s a call to action, inspiring us to rethink enterprise software and create the seamless future businesses deserve.

**Strategic Alignment**: Decisions get easier when you know what you're aiming for. This keeps resources and efforts locked on the right targets.
**Unity**: A shared future brings focus, rallying the team around one ambitious goal.

# Values (In Work)

**What** Core principles or beliefs that serves as a foundational element of the organization's culture, decision-making, and operations. Tenets are often concise, actionable statements that reflect the company's values and guide behavior within the organization.

**Why** Unlike mission or vision statements, which describe the company’s goals or future aspirations, tenets are enduring truths that help maintain consistency in how the company functions and interacts with stakeholders.

## **Values**
Nymbl believes that great companies can be built freely, unconstrained by politics and technology. We value doing the right thing above all else, that economics will follow. Our belief is that **transparency** is key and essential for communication and guiding action.

We value innovation backed by execution. Our employees are given authority, resources and trust. We align the company, team and individual objectives to do what’s best for clients and employees. We believe in decentralized management, where the successes and failures of teams are the product of iteration, collaboration and refinement. # Lucid Charts

### **What**
Cloud-based diagramming tool used for creating flowcharts, process maps, wireframes, UML diagrams, ERDs (Entity-Relationship Diagrams), and other types of visual documentation. It helps teams design, visualize, and collaborate on complex systems, workflows, and processes in real time.

### **Why**
Lucidchart is essential for a Nymbl because it allows:
*   **Clear Communication**: Visualizing complex systems, architectures, and workflows makes it easier to explain technical concepts to non-technical stakeholders, such as clients or product managers.
*   **Collaboration**: It enables teams to work together in real time, ensuring that developers, designers, and other stakeholders are on the same page, regardless of location.
*   **Efficiency**: Quick access to visual documentation like ERDs or workflows reduces ambiguity, helping developers to build features correctly the first time.
*   **Documentation**: Provides a visual record of the architecture and processes, which can be useful for onboarding new team members or future project iterations.
###   

### **Who**
*   **Developers and Solution Architects**: Use Lucidchart to design technical diagrams like database schemas, architecture diagrams, and component models.
*   **Product Managers**: Use it to create workflows and business process diagrams to help stakeholders understand the user journey and business logic.
*   **QA Engineers**: Utilize it for designing test cases or workflows to ensure comprehensive test coverage.
*   **Clients/Stakeholders**: Review diagrams to ensure the solution aligns with business goals and objectives.

  

### **When**
Lucidchart is used throughout the project lifecycle:
*   **During Initial Planning**: To design system architectures, workflows, and database models.
*   **User Story and Feature Development**: To visualize user flows and system interactions for specific features.
*   **Before Development**: To finalize the technical design with the development team and stakeholders.
*   **Throughout Development**: For ongoing updates to system diagrams and process maps, ensuring any changes are properly documented.
*   **Post-Launch**: To document the final architecture and workflows for future reference or iterations.

  

### **Helpful Websites & Links on How to Use Lucidchart**
*   **Lucidchart Learning Center** (Official Guide): Lucidchart Help Center
*   **Lucidchart YouTube Channel** (Video Tutorials): [Lucidchart YouTube Tutorials](https://www.youtube.com/user/Lucidchart)
*   **Lucidchart Blog** (Tips, Tricks, and Best Practices): Lucidchart Blog

Using Lucidchart effectively streamlines collaboration and ensures everyone has a clear, visual understanding of the project’s components, processes, and goals.

# Nymbl Glossary (in work)

### **What**
A centralized, living reference page that defines the terminology, acronyms, and role-specific language used across Nymbl.
It serves as the single source of truth for anyone who encounters an unfamiliar term — from internal tools and cultural tenets to client delivery and sales processes.
Each entry includes a **Term**, **What**, **Why**, and **Example** to make learning quick, contextual, and consistent.
### **Why**
Language is culture — and Nymbl moves fast. This Wiki ensures every team member, from new hires to senior leaders, can “speak Nymbl” with clarity and confidence.
It reduces onboarding time, eliminates miscommunication, and reinforces shared understanding across teams like Sales, Delivery, Engineering, and Client Success.
In short, it helps Nymbl stay aligned, precise, and connected — no matter how many projects or acronyms are flying around.

# TERMS

## **AOA — Analysis of Alternatives**
**What:** A structured comparison of multiple potential solutions against defined criteria like cost, time, complexity, and impact.
**Why:** Ensures that Nymbl recommends the most practical and value-aligned solution, not just the most interesting one.
**Example:** The Solution Architect conducts an AOA comparing Retool vs. custom React for a workflow automation MVP.
* * *
## **AR — Accounts Receivable**
**What:** Money owed to Nymbl by clients for completed work or issued invoices.
**Why:** Tracking AR ensures cash flow health and accountability for project billing.
**Example:** Finance confirms the AR for the Wolf & Co. engagement has been cleared before month-end.
* * *
## **AWS — Amazon Web Services**
**What:** A leading cloud services platform used by Nymbl to host, deploy, and scale applications.
**Why:** Provides secure, reliable, and cost-effective infrastructure for both client and internal solutions.
**Example:** The engineering team deploys a Retool backend API on AWS Lambda and stores assets in S3.
* * *
## **BANT — Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline**
**What:** A qualification framework for evaluating sales opportunities.
**Why:** Helps Nymbl’s Sales and BD teams focus on deals that are both real and ready.
**Example:** The SE marks a lead as “BANT-qualified” after confirming the client’s budget and project timeline.
* * *
## **BDR — Business Development Representative**
**What:** An outbound sales role responsible for generating and qualifying early-stage leads.
**Why:** BDRs fill the top of the funnel by identifying and nurturing potential clients.
**Example:** The BDR books an intro call for the CP after researching a qualified manufacturing prospect.
* * *
## **BQL — Business Qualified Lead**
**What:** A lead vetted by business criteria (fit, intent, and opportunity potential) before moving to sales qualification.
**Why:** Ensures marketing and business development teams pass only relevant leads into the pipeline.
**Example:** A lead that matches Nymbl’s ideal client profile and requests a demo becomes a BQL.
* * *
## **CEPBRIT — Nymbl Tenets Mnemonic**
**What:** A mnemonic for Nymbl’s core cultural tenets: **Curiosity, Empathy, Pragmatism, Boldness, Rigor, Integrity, Teamwork.**
**Why:** These values guide how Nymbl teams think, act, and collaborate internally and with clients.
**Example:** During a retro, the PM calls out how “Empathy” and “Rigor” showed up in client communications.
* * *
## **CO — Change Order**
**What:** A formal document used to modify scope, deliverables, or budget on an active SOW.
**Why:** Protects both Nymbl and the client by clearly documenting changes mid-project.
**Example:** When the client requests a new API integration, the PM issues a CO for approval.
* * *
## **CP — Client Partner**
**What:** The senior relationship owner responsible for client strategy, growth, and health across engagements.
**Why:** CPs connect delivery success with long-term client outcomes and expansion.
**Example:** Martyn (CP) leads the quarterly review with Darn Tough to discuss roadmap alignment.
* * *
## **CLM — Contract Lifecycle Management**
**What:** The process (and tools) for creating, approving, tracking, and storing contracts.
**Why:** Ensures version control, compliance, and timely renewals for all client agreements.
**Example:** The MSA and SOW documents are uploaded and tracked in Nymbl’s CLM repository.
* * *
## **CMS — Content Management System**
**What:** Software that enables non-technical users to create, manage, and update website content.
**Why:** Empowers clients and internal teams to manage content without engineering support.
**Example:** A client uses a headless CMS to update product pages in their WXP.
* * *
## **CRO — Conversion Rate Optimization**
**What:** The process of improving a website or funnel to increase the percentage of visitors who take desired actions.
**Why:** Helps Nymbl clients maximize ROI from their digital platforms.
**Example:** The WXP team A/B tests new landing page designs to improve trial sign-up conversion by 15%.
* * *
## **CSM — Client Success Manager**
**What:** The post-sale relationship manager ensuring clients achieve outcomes, renew successfully, and grow with Nymbl.
**Why:** Drives retention and expansion through proactive partnership and measurable value delivery.
**Example:** The CSM runs an SBR to review KPIs and discuss new opportunities for workflow automation.
* * *
## **CU — ClickUp**
**What:** Nymbl’s primary project and task management platform.
**Why:** Centralizes communication, tasks, milestones, and documentation across teams.
**Example:** The PM logs sprint progress and assigns QA tasks in ClickUp.
* * *
## **KPI — Key Performance Indicator**
**What:** A measurable value that indicates how effectively objectives are being met.
**Why:** Keeps teams accountable to outcomes rather than activity.
**Example:** A KPI for the Warranty App might be “Reduce claim processing time by 40%.”
* * *
## **LLM — Large Language Model**
**What:** An advanced AI model trained on massive text datasets to understand and generate human-like language.
**Why:** Enables automation of tasks such as writing, summarization, and customer support with natural, context-aware responses.
**Example:** The innovation team integrates an LLM into a client-facing support platform to provide instant, high-quality answers to user inquiries.
* * *
## **MQL — Marketing Qualified Lead**
**What:** A lead that has shown marketing engagement (downloads, demos, events) but hasn’t been vetted by sales yet.
**Why:** Helps measure marketing effectiveness and hand off warm leads to Sales.
**Example:** A manufacturing exec who downloads Nymbl’s WXP whitepaper becomes an MQL.
* * *
## **MSA — Master Services Agreement**
**What:** The legal framework governing all work with a client (indemnification, IP, insurance, etc.).
**Why:** Simplifies contracting — each new project only needs a new SOW under the same MSA.
**Example:** Nymbl signs an MSA with Cabot Hosiery Mills before executing multiple SOWs.
* * *
## **NDA — Non-Disclosure Agreement**
**What:** A confidentiality contract protecting shared information between Nymbl and clients.
**Why:** Enables open collaboration without risking intellectual property exposure.
**Example:** Nymbl signs an NDA before reviewing proprietary data with Wolf & Co.
* * *
## **NOS — Nymbl Operating System**
**What:** The shared set of playbooks, principles, and assets that guide how Nymbl works.
**Why:** Creates consistency and excellence across discovery, delivery, and enablement.
**Example:** The SA references the NOS during a project kickoff to align the team on approach.
* * *
## **OCR — Optical Character Recognition**
**What:** Technology that converts printed or handwritten text in images or scanned documents into machine-readable text.
**Why:** Streamlines data entry, improves accessibility, and enables text search within scanned files or photos.
**Example:** The operations team applies OCR to extract client information from scanned contracts, reducing manual data entry time.
* * *
## **PO — Purchase Order**
**What:** A client-issued document authorizing Nymbl to deliver services and invoice.
**Why:** Required for billing and financial control, especially with enterprise clients.
**Example:** Finance confirms a valid PO before sending the first invoice for a T&M project.
* * *
## **POC — Proof of Concept**
**What:** A small-scale build used to validate a concept or approach before full development.
**Why:** Reduces technical and business risk by testing feasibility early.
**Example:** The SE develops a Retool POC showing live data sync from M3.
* * *
## **PM — Project Manager**
**What:** The role responsible for planning, execution, communication, and delivery of client projects.
**Why:** Keeps teams aligned and ensures commitments are met on time and budget.
**Example:** The PM facilitates sprint retros and manages timelines in ClickUp.
* * *
## **PR — Payment Requisitioner**
**What:** The internal Nymbl role or person who requests invoice creation after a milestone or deliverable is complete.
**Why:** Ensures timely and accurate billing for client projects.
**Example:** Once the MVP is delivered, the PM notifies the PR to initiate the invoice.
* * *
## **QA — Quality Assurance**
**What:** The process of verifying that software meets functional, usability, and reliability standards.
**Why:** Ensures client confidence by catching issues before production.
**Example:** QA tests a Retool dashboard workflow for performance and data integrity.
* * *
## **RAG — Retrieval Augmented Generation**
**What:** A method that combines large language models with external data retrieval to generate more accurate and up-to-date responses.
**Why:** Ensures AI-generated outputs are grounded in verified, real-world information rather than relying solely on model memory.
**Example:** The data science team uses RAG to power an internal chatbot that pulls live company policy data before answering employee questions.
* * *
## **Retool PS — Retool Professional Services**
**What:** Retool’s internal services arm that supports clients in implementing and customizing the Retool platform.
**Why:** Nymbl often partners or aligns with Retool PS for advanced enterprise solutions.
**Example:** The SA references a Retool PS best practice when designing app layouts.
* * *
## **RM — Resource Management**
**What:** The process of allocating team members across active and upcoming projects.
**Why:** Balances workloads and ensures optimal utilization of skills.
**Example:** RM reviews capacity reports before staffing a new engagement.
* * *
## **SA — Solution Architect**
**What:** The technical strategist who defines the “how” — designing systems, integrations, and architecture.
**Why:** Ensures scalable, maintainable, and efficient solutions that meet business needs.
**Example:** The SA maps out integrations between Fieldguide and Lunar AI.
* * *
## **SE — Sales Engineer**
**What:** The technical partner to the sales team responsible for demos, scoping, and solution design in pre-sales.
**Why:** Bridges business requirements and technical feasibility early in the sales process.
**Example:** The SE builds a quick prototype to show how Nymbl could automate warranty approvals.
* * *
## **SEO — Search Engine Optimization**
**What:** The practice of improving a website’s visibility in search engines like Google.
**Why:** Increases organic traffic and lead generation for clients’ digital platforms.
**Example:** The WXP team optimizes on-page SEO for a client’s product catalog to rank higher.
* * *
## **SOW — Statement of Work**
**What:** The document outlining specific project scope, deliverables, timeline, and cost.
**Why:** Defines exactly what Nymbl will deliver under an MSA.
**Example:** A new SOW covers the enhancement phase of the Darn Tough Warranty Portal.
* * *
## **SQL — Sales Qualified Lead**
**What:** A lead vetted by sales as a real opportunity ready for direct engagement.
**Why:** Marks the handoff point between marketing qualification and formal pursuit.
**Example:** After a discovery call confirming need and budget, the lead becomes an SQL.
* * *
## **SS — Software Subscription**
**What:** Ongoing license or access fee for using software, billed monthly or annually.
**Why:** Part of Nymbl’s recurring revenue model and client enablement offerings.
**Example:** A client pays an annual SS for access to Nymbl’s analytics module.
* * *
## **SBR — Stakeholder Business Review**
**What:** A recurring meeting (typically quarterly) to review client goals, outcomes, and roadmap alignment.
**Why:** Builds executive relationships and reinforces business value delivery.
**Example:** The CSM leads an SBR showcasing ROI metrics and new initiative proposals.
* * *
## **SME — Subject Matter Expert**
**What:** A person with deep expertise in a particular process, system, or domain.
**Why:** SMEs ensure technical or business accuracy during solution design and delivery.
**Example:** A tax compliance SME advises on logic for the Wolf & Co. engagement setup.
* * *
## **T&M — Time & Materials**
**What:** A billing model where clients are charged for actual hours worked and materials used.
**Why:** Provides flexibility for evolving projects with uncertain scope.
**Example:** Nymbl uses T&M for early-stage discovery before finalizing a fixed-price proposal.
* * *
## **WGLL — What Good Looks Like (“wig-gle”)**
**What:** A Nymbl standard describing the ideal version of a process, deliverable, or behavior.
**Why:** Sets clear expectations and drives consistency in quality.
**Example:** The SA references the WGLL for “Use Case Documentation” before client playback.
* * *
## **WBLL — What Bad Looks Like (“wob-ble”)**
**What:** The opposite of WGLL — examples of missteps or poor practices to avoid.
**Why:** Reinforces clarity by showing contrast between effective and ineffective outcomes.
**Example:** The PM shares a WBLL example of unclear acceptance criteria to coach new hires.
* * *
## **WXP — Website Experience Platform**
**What:** Nymbl’s modern web solution model combining CMS, CRO, and analytics into one cohesive system.
**Why:** Helps clients deliver flexible, measurable digital experiences.
**Example:** The WXP team launches a unified content and e-commerce experience for a retail client.
